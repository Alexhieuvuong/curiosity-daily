# The "Ghost" Economy of Recycled Cooking Oil in Lagos

> What if your dinner was fried in oil that has already been used three times — collected from restaurant drains, filtered in a back alley, and resold under a different name?

## Why this is interesting
The informal economy is often assumed to be about street vendors or unregistered labor. But one of its most revealing microcosms is the trade in used cooking oil (UCO) in a megacity like Lagos. It shows how a hazardous waste product is transformed into a profitable commodity through a completely unregulated, trust-based network — and how that network connects directly to your health, the environment, and global commodity markets.

## The core mechanic
In any city with many restaurants, fryers produce vast quantities of used oil. Instead of paying for disposal (as formal businesses do), a chain of informal collectors — often on motorcycles or pushcarts — buys this oil for a tiny fee. They then filter it, sometimes mix it with fresh oil or chemicals to remove smell and color, and sell it to smaller food vendors (who cannot afford fresh oil) or to producers of cheap soap and animal feed. The key is that no one verifies quality or safety; the entire transaction is cash-based and word-of-mouth. The price of a liter of "recycled" oil is roughly half the price of fresh oil, creating a powerful economic incentive for everyone except the end consumer.

## Break it into pieces
- **Where does the oil come from?** — Not just restaurants, but also airports, hotel kitchens, and fast-food chains. The more formal the source, the more likely it is to have a legal disposal contract — but leakage into the informal market is common.
- **How is it "processed"?** — The range is vast: simple straining through a cloth, boiling with bleaching earth, or adding caustic soda to neutralize free fatty acids. Most processes remove visible impurities but not harmful compounds like trans-fats or aldehydes.
- **Who buys it?** — Small-scale food vendors (especially those selling fried snacks, akara, or plantain chips), animal feed producers, and soap makers. Each buyer knows the oil is recycled but trusts the collector's reputation.
- **How do you measure a "ghost" market?** — It is nearly impossible to measure directly. Economists use proxies: comparing cooking oil imports with estimated consumption, or tracking hospital admissions for gastro-intestinal issues near street food hubs.

## Follow the incentives
- **The collector** — earns a margin on volume. They pay the restaurant ~10% of the fresh oil price, then sell to vendors at ~50% of the fresh price. Their profit is the difference, minus the cost of their time and a bucket. They bear the risk of theft or police confiscation.
- **The restaurant** — avoids paying for oil disposal (which costs ~$0.10–0.20/liter in formal waste management). They also get a small cash payment. Their incentive is purely cost-saving.
- **The food vendor** — gets cheap cooking oil. Their profit margin on fried food is already razor-thin; using fresh oil would push them into loss. They bear the health risk themselves — but they don't feel it immediately.
- **The state** — loses tax revenue (the oil is untaxed), pays for health costs from foodborne illness, and lacks enforcement capacity. Their incentive to act is weak because the market is diffuse and voters are the vendors.

## Second-order effects
- **Health burden falls on the poor** — The poorest eaters, who buy the cheapest street food, consume the most recycled oil. This creates a hidden subsidy: the rich avoid the health costs of bad oil, while the poor absorb them.
- **Environmental paradox** — This informal recycling actually reduces waste: used oil is collected and reused (as soap, feed, or fuel) rather than poured down drains. If formal regulation cracked down too hard, the oil might end up in sewers, causing worse pollution.
- **Quality deterioration in the long run** — As the market grows, trust degrades. Some collectors start mixing used oil with mineral oil or other adulterants, creating a race to the bottom. The most honest collectors struggle to compete with the most dishonest ones.
- **A seed for formalization** — Some cities (like Shanghai or Jakarta) have tried to formalize this trade by licensing collectors and requiring testing. In Lagos, a few NGOs have started pilot programs to turn UCO into biodiesel, which requires traceability — but the informal market resists transparency because it is its main competitive advantage.

## A question to sit with
What would happen if the government suddenly enforced a strict ban on the sale of unrefined used cooking oil — would public health improve, or would the market simply go deeper underground, making the oil even more dangerous?

## Go deeper
- **The "gutter oil" scandal in China (2010–2012)** — A massive case where recycled cooking oil was sold to major fast-food chains and hotels, leading to arrests and a public health crisis. It shows how a formal market can be infiltrated by the informal one.
- **Biodiesel and the global UCO trade** — The European Union imports millions of tons of used cooking oil from Asia (mostly China and Indonesia) to make biodiesel. This has created a global commodity market with its own traceability problems.
- **The role of trust networks in informal economies** — How do collectors build reputation without contracts? In Lagos, it is often based on neighborhood, ethnicity, or a family connection. Read about "moral economy" in the work of E.P. Thompson or James C. Scott.
