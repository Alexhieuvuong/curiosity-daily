> The majority of the world's workers operate outside government oversight—not because they are criminals, but because the formal economy has no room for them. [4]

## Why this is interesting
For decades, the informal economy was treated as a problem to be solved—a sign of underdevelopment, tax evasion, or lawlessness. Yet 61.2% of the world's employed population—two billion workers—are in informal employment [4]. This is not a fringe phenomenon; it is the most common mode of work on the planet. Understanding why so many people remain "off the books" is a key policy challenge [1].

## First principles
An informal economy is any economic activity that is neither taxed nor monitored by a government [1]. This includes everything from a street vendor selling fruit without a license to a small factory producing goods without registering employees. The informal sector provides critical economic opportunities for the poor [1] and most people enter it not by choice, but due to a lack of opportunities in the formal economy [4]. The informal sector overlaps considerably with the formal economy [2], and it contributes substantially to employment and economic growth in many developing countries [2].

## Break it into pieces
- **Choice vs. necessity:** Most people enter the informal economy not by choice, but because the formal economy offers no opportunities [4].
- **Measurement problems:** Because informal activity is unrecorded, official GDP figures systematically underestimate economic output. Italy added 18% to its GDP by including estimates of informal activity [1].
- **Worker protections vs. economic dynamism:** Informal workers lack protections, but informal firms are often more flexible, competitive, and innovative than their formal counterparts [2].
- **The criminal boundary:** The informal economy includes both legitimate work (unregistered vendors) and illicit activity (prostitution, drug sales). Some European countries now include the latter in official GDP [1].

## Follow the incentives
Integrating the informal economy into the formal sector is an important policy challenge [1]. In China, local governments have adopted policies designed to encourage the informal economy to alleviate unemployment [2]. For the two billion people in informal employment [4], most enter not by choice, but due to a lack of opportunities in the formal economy. China's rise as a global center of manufacturing owes, in part, to its willingness to trade in the informal economy, also known as system D trade—producing unregulated goods for consumption in Africa, Latin America, and elsewhere [2].

## How it echoes elsewhere
Illustrative: The same tension between formal rules and practical survival appears in **urban planning**: squatter settlements (favelas, slums) are illegal but house millions because formal housing is unaffordable. Illustrative: Another echo is in **intellectual property**: "piracy" of software and media is often the only way people in low-income countries can access digital tools.

## A real-world case
In Pakistan, a lower-middle-income country with a population exceeding 250 million and one of the lowest GDP per capita outputs in the world [3], the formal economy's limited capacity to absorb labor is a persistent challenge. The Pakistani rupee's sustained depreciation and recurring debt crises [3] strain public finances, which can limit the formal economy's ability to absorb labor.

## Second-order effects
When two billion workers are informal [4], policy frameworks built on formal employment may not reach the majority of workers. Because informal activity is not included in official GDP figures [1], economic statistics may understate true output. China's rise as a global center of manufacturing owes, in part, to its willingness to trade in the informal economy [2].

## A question to sit with
If the formal economy cannot provide livelihoods for two billion people, is the problem informality—or is the problem the formal economy itself?
