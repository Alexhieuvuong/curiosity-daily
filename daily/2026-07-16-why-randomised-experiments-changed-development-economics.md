# Why Randomised Experiments Changed Development Economics

> The same tool that tests a new drug can test whether a school feeding program works — and the results upended decades of development policy.

## Why this is interesting
Most economic advice for poor countries used to come from theory, historical case studies, or gut instinct. Then a wave of researchers started running randomised controlled trials (RCTs) in villages and slums, treating anti-poverty programs like clinical trials. The approach sparked both excitement and fierce criticism, revealing deep tensions about what counts as evidence in economics.

## First principles
Development economics studies how low- and middle-income countries grow, reduce poverty, and improve health, education, and working conditions [1]. Unlike mainstream economics, it often incorporates social and political factors and lacks consensus on what students should know [1]. A randomised experiment randomly assigns people to a treatment group (who get a program) or a control group (who don't), then compares outcomes — isolating the program's effect from other factors [5]. The key innovation is that researchers work closely with implementers (NGOs, governments) to design the experiment, allowing estimation of parameters that would not otherwise be possible to evaluate [5].

## Break it into pieces
- **Why randomise?** Without random assignment, you can't tell if a program caused an outcome or if better-off people simply chose to participate.
- **What can it measure?** Concrete, short-term effects: Did test scores rise? Did children get vaccinated? Did farmers adopt new seeds?
- **What can't it measure?** Long-run structural change, general equilibrium effects (e.g., what happens when everyone in a region gets the same program), or why something works.
- **Why the controversy?** Critics say RCTs are expensive, context-specific, and can't answer big questions about institutions, power, or macroeconomic reform.
- **The middle ground:** Proponents acknowledge limitations but argue they are not unique to experiments — all methods have flaws [5].

## Follow the incentives
Researchers want to publish credible results; RCTs offer high internal validity, making them attractive for top journals. Funders (World Bank, Gates Foundation, USAID) want to know which programs actually work before spending billions. NGOs and governments gain political cover: they can say "this program is evidence-based." The risk falls on the poor communities who may receive a placebo-like control condition — receiving no intervention while the treatment group gets help. Researchers bear reputational risk if results fail to replicate or if ethical concerns arise.

## How it echoes elsewhere
Illustrative: The same logic drives medical clinical trials — the gold standard for drug approval. In agricultural economics, field experiments test whether new seeds or fertiliser techniques boost yields [3]. The core pattern is the same: isolate cause and effect by controlling who gets the treatment, then scale what works.

## A real-world case
Illustrative: In the 2000s, researchers ran an RCT in Kenya testing whether providing deworming pills to schoolchildren reduced school absenteeism. The treatment group received free deworming; the control group did not. Results showed deworming significantly reduced absenteeism — at a cost of just a few dollars per extra year of schooling. This finding influenced policy worldwide and became one of the most-cited examples in development economics. (This case is well-documented in the experimental economics literature, consistent with the framework described in [5].)

## Second-order effects
- **Shift in funding:** Illustrative: Donors now demand RCT evidence before funding programs, potentially crowding out research on systemic reforms (trade policy, land rights) that can't be randomised.
- **Ethical dilemmas:** If a program is believed to work, is it ethical to deny it to a control group? Some argue it is, because resources are scarce and we need to know what works.
- **Simplification risk:** RCTs favour measurable, short-term outcomes (test scores, vaccinations) over harder-to-measure but potentially more important ones (institutional quality, political empowerment).
- **Replication crisis:** As more RCTs are run, some results fail to replicate in different contexts, raising questions about external validity.

## A question to sit with
If a randomised experiment shows that a program works in one village in India, should a government in a different country adopt it without further testing — or does the very specificity of the method limit its usefulness for policy?

## Go deeper
- Compare RCTs with "natural experiments" — events like policy changes that create quasi-random assignment.
- Explore the "credibility revolution" in economics: how better methods have changed what we think we know.
- Debate whether development economics should focus on "what works" locally or on grand theories of growth and structural transformation.

## Vocabulary Builder

1. **randomised controlled trial** — (noun phrase, /ˈrændəmaɪzd kənˈtroʊld ˈtraɪəl/) — An experiment where subjects are randomly assigned to treatment and control groups to measure causal effects. _Example: The randomised controlled trial showed that deworming pills reduced school absenteeism by 25 percent._
2. **internal validity** — (noun phrase, /ɪnˈtɜːrnəl vəˈlɪdəti/) — The degree to which an experiment accurately measures a causal relationship. _Example: Randomised experiments have high internal validity because they eliminate selection bias._
3. **external validity** — (noun phrase, /ɪkˈstɜːrnəl vəˈlɪdəti/) — The degree to which results from one study can be generalised to other settings or populations. _Example: Critics question the external validity of a single village experiment for national policy._
4. **treatment group** — (noun phrase, /ˈtriːtmənt ɡruːp/) — The set of subjects who receive the intervention being tested. _Example: The treatment group received free textbooks, while the control group did not._
5. **control group** — (noun phrase, /kənˈtroʊl ɡruːp/) — The set of subjects who do not receive the intervention, used as a baseline for comparison. _Example: The control group continued with the standard curriculum to provide a benchmark._
6. **selection bias** — (noun phrase, /sɪˈlekʃən ˈbaɪəs/) — Systematic error caused by non-random assignment of subjects to groups. _Example: Without randomisation, selection bias could make a program look effective when motivated families chose to participate._
7. **parameter** — (noun, /pəˈræmɪtər/) — A measurable factor that defines a system or determines its behaviour. _Example: The experiment estimated the parameter of how much a subsidy increased vaccination rates._
8. **implementer** — (noun, /ˈɪmplɪmɛntər/) — An organisation or person that carries out a program or policy in the field. _Example: The researchers collaborated closely with the NGO implementer to design the experiment._
9. **general equilibrium** — (noun phrase, /ˈdʒɛnərəl ˌiːkwɪˈlɪbriəm/) — A state where all markets in an economy simultaneously clear, accounting for feedback effects. _Example: A randomised experiment cannot capture general equilibrium effects if the program changes local wages._
10. **structural change** — (noun phrase, /ˈstrʌktʃərəl tʃeɪndʒ/) — A fundamental shift in the composition of an economy, e.g., from agriculture to industry. _Example: Randomised trials are better at measuring program impacts than at explaining structural change._
11. **credibility revolution** — (noun phrase, /ˌkrɛdɪˈbɪləti ˌrɛvəˈluːʃən/) — The movement in empirical economics toward using research designs that credibly identify causal effects. _Example: The credibility revolution pushed development economists to adopt randomised experiments._
12. **replication** — (noun, /ˌrɛplɪˈkeɪʃən/) — Repeating a study in a different setting to confirm its findings. _Example: Replication of the deworming experiment in other countries showed mixed results._
