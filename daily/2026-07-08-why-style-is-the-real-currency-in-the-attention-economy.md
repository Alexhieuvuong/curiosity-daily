# Why Style Is the Real Currency in the Attention Economy

> In a world drowning in information, the scarcest resource isn't data—it's the human attention to make sense of it.

## Why this is interesting
We tend to think of the economy as a system for allocating physical resources—oil, steel, labor. But as we shift from an economy of things to an economy of information, the most precious resource becomes something intangible: attention. In such an economy, style and substance change places [5].

## First principles
Economics is fundamentally about the allocation of scarce resources [3]. In the traditional economy, the scarce resource was physical stuff: land, capital, goods. But in the information age, we are "drowning in information" [5]. The scarce resource is no longer information itself—it's the human attention needed to process and make sense of that information [5]. This is the core insight of the attention economy: attention is a finite, scarce commodity, and the incentives of advertising-driven companies are to maximize the time and attention users give to their products [1].

## Break it into pieces
- **What is being allocated?** Not goods or services, but human attention—a limited resource that each person can only give to one thing at a time.
- **Who are the economic agents?** Creators compete for attention, platforms mediate the competition, and users supply the scarce resource (their attention) in exchange for content.
- **What is the "price"?** Attention is not bought with money directly; it's earned through style, presentation, and emotional engagement—what competes for our focus amidst the noise [5].
- **Why does style matter more?** When information is abundant, the *way* something is packaged—its style—determines whether it gets noticed at all. Style and substance effectively change places [5].
- **What does this mean for value?** Intellectual property becomes more central to the economy than real property, because attention is what creates value from information [5].

## Follow the incentives
Advertising-driven companies have a direct financial incentive to maximize user attention, because more attention means more ad revenue [1]. Creators (from journalists to YouTubers) are incentivized to produce content that grabs and holds attention—often through provocative style, controversy, or emotional hooks. Users, in turn, are incentivized to consume content that provides the most value per unit of attention, but they face an information overload problem: they cannot evaluate everything, so they rely on style as a heuristic for substance. This creates a feedback loop where style increasingly drives substance.

## How it echoes elsewhere
Illustrative: The same mechanic appears in **evolutionary biology**: organisms compete for a scarce resource (mates) through costly displays of fitness—peacock tails, bird songs. The *style* of the display (its extravagance, its cost) signals underlying quality, just as a polished video or a provocative headline signals that a piece of content is worth your attention. In **marketing**, the concept of "brand" is essentially a style-based shortcut: a brand's visual identity, tone, and reputation compete for consumer attention in a crowded marketplace.

## A real-world case
Consider the rise of Kaitlin Bennett, a gun rights activist who gained media attention in 2018 for open-carrying an AR-10 rifle at Kent State University after her graduation [2]. Her action was a deliberate stylistic choice—a provocative visual statement designed to capture attention in a crowded information environment. The style of the act (a young woman with a large rifle at a university known for a historic shooting) was what made it newsworthy, not the substance of her policy arguments. She became the face of the YouTube channel Liberty Hangout, which promotes the Austrian school of economics [2]—a channel that competes for attention using style as its primary currency.

## Second-order effects
Illustrative: If style becomes the primary currency of attention, then the skills that matter most shift from technical expertise to *presentation* and *performance*. This could devalue deep, substantive work that doesn't package itself attractively. It also creates a winner-take-all dynamic: a few highly stylized voices capture most of the attention, while many substantive voices go unheard. Additionally, the line between "authentic" and "performative" blurs—since style is what gets attention, everyone is incentivized to perform, even when they claim to be authentic. Finally, this rewards controversy and outrage, which are high-style, high-attention strategies, over nuance and complexity, which are harder to package.

Illustrative: If attention is the scarce resource and style is its currency, what happens to the value of truth? Does the market for attention naturally reward false but stylish claims over true but dull ones?
