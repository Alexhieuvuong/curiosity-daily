# The Tension in Defining Money: Why Economists Keep Changing the Rules

> Money is whatever we *say* it is—but the people saying it keep disagreeing on what that means.

## Why this is interesting

The history of money is not a smooth story of progress from cowrie shells to credit cards. It is a story of conceptual tension: economists, anthropologists, and historians have spent decades arguing over what money *really* is, because the definition itself shapes how we understand entire economies. A 1959 study of African subsistence economies revealed that when economists looked at non-Western societies, they suddenly abandoned the very criteria they used to define money in their own world [5]. This double standard exposes a deep, unresolved puzzle at the heart of economic theory.

## First principles

Money is any item or verifiable record that is generally accepted as payment for goods and services and repayment of debts [2]. Its primary functions are: medium of exchange, unit of account, store of value, and sometimes standard of deferred payment [2]. Historically, money emerged as a market phenomenon with intrinsic value (commodity money), but nearly all modern systems use fiat money—currency declared legal tender by a government, with value derived from social convention rather than use value [2]. The money supply includes physical currency and, more importantly, bank money (balances in checking and savings accounts), which forms the largest part of broad money in developed countries [2].

## Break it into pieces

- **Commodity vs. fiat money**: Does money need intrinsic value, or is social agreement enough? The shift from gold coins to unbacked paper currency shows how the answer has changed over centuries [1][2].
- **Functions as a moving target**: Economists assign three or four functions to money when discussing modern societies, but when studying ancient or non-Western economies, they often focus on only one—say, medium of exchange—and ignore the others [5].
- **Who gets to define money?**: Governments declare legal tender, but market participants create bank money and digital records. The definition is negotiated between state authority and private practice [2].
- **The "trick of definition" problem**: Claims that pre-colonial African societies had money often evaporate under scrutiny, because scholars stretch or shrink the definition to fit their narrative [5].

## Follow the incentives

Governments profit from controlling money because legal tender status forces citizens to accept it for taxes and debts, giving the state a powerful tool for managing the economy [2]. Banks profit from creating bank money (deposits) because it expands the money supply beyond physical currency, allowing them to lend more than they hold in reserves [2]. Academics profit from publishing papers that challenge or refine definitions—this is how careers are built. The 1959 paper on African subsistence economies explicitly notes that economists' inconsistent definitions serve their own analytical convenience, not objective reality [5].

## How it echoes elsewhere

The same tension appears in the definition of a "firm." In his 1937 paper *The Nature of the Firm*, Ronald Coase pointed out that economists often use a definition of "firm" that differs from the "plain man's" understanding, and that choosing between rival assumptions is essential for clear theory [4]. Just as with money, the definition of a firm is not a neutral description—it shapes what questions get asked and what conclusions are drawn.

## A real-world case

Illustrative: A 1959 study of African subsistence economies argued that claims of pre-colonial money systems often "evaporate or to emerge as tricks of definition" when examined closely [5]. The paper noted that economists apply different criteria for money when studying non-Western societies compared to modern ones—sometimes focusing on only one function, such as medium of exchange, rather than the full set of three or four functions used for contemporary economies [5]. This inconsistency reveals that the very criteria for "money" are culturally loaded—what looks like money to an anthropologist may not look like money to an economist, and vice versa.

## Second-order effects

If economists cannot agree on what money is, then policies built on those definitions—like measuring inflation, setting interest rates, or regulating digital currencies—rest on shaky foundations. The rise of cryptocurrencies and stablecoins has revived this old debate: are they money, or just speculative assets? The answer depends on which functions you prioritize. Furthermore, the historical inconsistency in defining money for non-Western societies may have justified colonial economic policies that dismissed indigenous systems as "primitive" or nonexistent, with lasting consequences for development.

## A question to sit with

If money is defined by its functions, and those functions change depending on who is looking and why, is money a stable category at all—or is it whatever powerful institutions say it is at any given moment?

## Vocabulary Builder

1. **intrinsic value** — (noun phrase, /ɪnˈtrɪnzɪk ˈvæljuː/) — the inherent worth of an object itself, independent of its use as money. *Example: Gold coins have intrinsic value because the metal can be used for jewelry or electronics, unlike paper banknotes.*
2. **fiat money** — (noun, /ˈfiːæt ˈmʌni/) — currency that a government has declared to be legal tender, but is not backed by a physical commodity. *Example: Most modern economies use fiat money, whose value depends on public trust and government decree.*
3. **legal tender** — (noun, /ˈliːɡəl ˈtendər/) — a form of money that must be accepted for payment of debts, as specified by law. *Example: The U.S. dollar is legal tender for all debts, public and private.*
4. **medium of exchange** — (noun phrase, /ˈmiːdiəm əv ɪksˈtʃeɪndʒ/) — an intermediary instrument used to facilitate the sale, purchase, or trade of goods. *Example: A currency's primary function is to serve as a medium of exchange, replacing the inefficiencies of barter.*
5. **unit of account** — (noun phrase, /ˈjuːnɪt əv əˈkaʊnt/) — a standard monetary unit of measurement of value/cost of goods, services, or assets. *Example: The dollar acts as a unit of account when prices are listed in dollars rather than in hours of labor.*
6. **store of value** — (noun phrase, /stɔːr əv ˈvæljuː/) — the function of an asset that can be saved, retrieved, and exchanged at a later time. *Example: Gold has historically served as a store of value, preserving purchasing power over long periods.*
7. **subsistence economy** — (noun, /səbˈsɪstəns ɪˈkɒnəmi/) — an economy in which goods are produced for personal consumption rather than for trade. *Example: The Tiv people operated a subsistence economy where most goods were consumed locally rather than sold.*
8. **multicentric economy** — (noun, /ˌmʌltiˈsentrɪk ɪˈkɒnəmi/) — an economic system with multiple, separate spheres of exchange, each with its own currency. *Example: Bohannan argued that the Tiv had a multicentric economy, with different currencies for food, prestige goods, and marriage payments.*
9. **double standard** — (noun, /ˈdʌbəl ˈstændərd/) — a rule or principle applied unfairly to different groups or situations. *Example: The 1959 paper exposed a double standard: economists used strict criteria for modern money but loose ones for African systems.*
10. **evaporate under scrutiny** — (phrase, /ɪˈvæpəreɪt ˈʌndər ˈskruːtəni/) — to disappear or be disproven when examined closely. *Example: Claims that pre-colonial Africa had money often evaporate under scrutiny when you apply consistent definitions.*
11. **conceptual tension** — (noun, /kənˈseptʃuəl ˈtenʃən/) — a conflict or inconsistency between ideas or definitions. *Example: The conceptual tension between commodity and fiat money has shaped centuries of economic debate.*
12. **shaky foundations** — (noun phrase, /ˈʃeɪki faʊnˈdeɪʃənz/) — an unstable or unreliable basis for reasoning or policy. *Example: If economists disagree on what money is, policies built on those definitions rest on shaky foundations.*
