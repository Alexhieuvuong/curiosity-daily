# Why Healthcare Doesn't Follow Normal Economic Rules

> In most markets, higher prices signal better quality—but in healthcare, the person choosing the treatment isn't the one paying for it.

## Why this is interesting

Healthcare is one of the few sectors where the fundamental assumptions of classical economics break down almost entirely. The usual mechanisms of price discovery, consumer choice, and market efficiency are replaced by a web of third-party payers, asymmetric information, and life-or-death decisions. Understanding why healthcare defies normal economics reveals deep tensions in how we value health itself.

## First principles

Health economics is a branch of economics concerned with efficiency, effectiveness, value, and behavior in the production and consumption of health and healthcare [1]. A seminal 1963 article by Kenneth Arrow is often credited with giving rise to health economics as a discipline, drawing conceptual distinctions between health and other goods [1]. The core difficulty is that healthcare does not follow normal rules for economics: price and quality are often hidden by the third-party payer system of insurance companies and employers [1]. Additionally, Quality Adjusted Life Years (QALYs)—one of the most commonly used measurements for treatments—is very difficult to measure and relies upon assumptions that are often unreasonable [1].

## Break it into pieces

- **Third-party agency problem**: Who is the customer? The patient consumes the care, but the insurer pays for it. This creates misaligned incentives between all three parties [1].
- **Asymmetric information**: Doctors know far more about what treatments are needed than patients do, making informed consumer choice nearly impossible [1].
- **Externalities everywhere**: Getting a flu shot protects not just you but everyone around you—meaning individual decisions about health have collective consequences that markets fail to price [1].
- **Intractable uncertainty**: You cannot know in advance whether you will need surgery next year, making insurance essential but also distorting how care is consumed [1].

## Follow the incentives

Patients want the best possible care with minimal out-of-pocket cost—they have little reason to price-shop when insurance covers most expenses. Insurers (the third-party agents) want to minimize their payouts while keeping premiums competitive, so they restrict coverage and negotiate lower prices with providers [1]. Providers (hospitals, doctors) want to maximize revenue while maintaining quality and reputation, which can lead to over-treatment or upcoding. Government intervenes extensively because of the massive externalities—infectious disease control, opioid abuse, public health emergencies—that private markets alone cannot handle [1].

## How it echoes elsewhere

Illustrative: The same third-party agency problem appears in legal services paid by insurance or in car repairs when an insurance company foots the bill. In both cases, the person consuming the service has little incentive to minimize cost, while the payer has limited ability to verify what was actually necessary. The pattern is universal: whenever a decision-maker is separated from the financial consequences of their choices, market efficiency degrades.

## A real-world case

Consider the opioid epidemic in the United States. Externalities arise frequently when considering health and health care, notably in the context of the health impacts as with infectious disease or opioid abuse [1]. An individual's decision to misuse prescription opioids affects not only themselves but also their family, community, and healthcare system. The market failed to price these externalities, leading to widespread addiction, overdose deaths, and billions in societal costs that no single transaction captured. Finding sustainable, humane and effective solutions to such problems requires going beyond simple market fixes [1].

## Second-order effects

Because healthcare markets are so distorted, governments and insurers have developed complex administrative apparatuses—prior authorization, formularies, utilization review—that themselves consume enormous resources. The U.S. social expenditure stood at roughly 30% of GDP [2]. Another second-order effect is that innovation incentives become skewed: companies invest heavily in blockbuster drugs that can command high prices through insurance coverage, while under-investing in simple preventive measures that would save more lives but generate less revenue. Finally, the difficulty of measuring QALYs means that cost-effectiveness analysis can become politicized, with different groups arguing over whose assumptions about the value of a year of life are "correct."

## A question to sit with

If healthcare cannot follow normal market rules, what alternative mechanism should determine how much we spend on treating a disease versus preventing it—and who gets to decide?

## Vocabulary Builder

1. **third-party payer** — (noun phrase, /θɜːrd ˈpɑːrti ˈpeɪər/) — An entity (such as an insurance company) that pays for healthcare services on behalf of the patient. *Example: The third-party payer system hides the true price of medical care from the patient.*
2. **asymmetric information** — (noun phrase, /ˌeɪsɪˈmɛtrɪk ˌɪnfərˈmeɪʃən/) — A situation where one party in a transaction has more or better information than the other. *Example: Asymmetric information between doctors and patients makes informed consumer choice nearly impossible.*
3. **externality** — (noun, /ˌɛkstɜːrˈnælɪti/) — A cost or benefit that affects a third party who did not choose to incur that cost or benefit. *Example: Getting vaccinated creates a positive externality by protecting others from infection.*
4. **QALY** — (noun, /ˈkwɑːli/) — Quality-Adjusted Life Year, a measure of disease burden that combines quantity and quality of life. *Example: QALYs are commonly used to compare the cost-effectiveness of different medical treatments.*
5. **intractable uncertainty** — (noun phrase, /ɪnˈtræktəbl ʌnˈsɜːrtənti/) — Uncertainty that cannot be easily reduced or eliminated through information gathering. *Example: The intractable uncertainty of future health needs makes insurance essential but also distorts healthcare markets.*
6. **moral hazard** — (noun phrase, /ˈmɔːrəl ˈhæzərd/) — The tendency for insurance to increase the likelihood of the insured event because the insured bears less of the cost. *Example: When patients pay little out-of-pocket, moral hazard can lead to overuse of healthcare services.*
7. **cost-effectiveness analysis** — (noun phrase, /kɒst ɪˈfɛktɪvnəs əˈnælɪsɪs/) — A method for comparing the relative costs and outcomes of different interventions. *Example: Cost-effectiveness analysis helps policymakers decide which treatments to fund with limited resources.*
8. **utilization review** — (noun phrase, /ˌjuːtɪlɪˈzeɪʃən rɪˈvjuː/) — A process insurers use to evaluate whether medical services are necessary and appropriate. *Example: Utilization review can deny coverage for treatments the insurer deems unnecessary.*
9. **upcoding** — (noun, /ʌpˈkoʊdɪŋ/) — The practice of billing for a more expensive service than was actually provided. *Example: Hospitals may engage in upcoding to maximize revenue from insurance reimbursements.*
10. **blockbuster drug** — (noun phrase, /ˈblɒkˌbʌstər drʌɡ/) — A highly profitable medication that generates annual sales of at least $1 billion. *Example: The patent system incentivizes investment in blockbuster drugs over cheaper preventive treatments.*
11. **administrative apparatus** — (noun phrase, /ədˈmɪnɪstrətɪv ˌæpəˈreɪtəs/) — The complex system of rules, forms, and procedures used to manage an organization. *Example: The administrative apparatus of prior authorization consumes significant time and resources.*
12. **preventive measure** — (noun phrase, /prɪˈvɛntɪv ˈmɛʒər/) — An action taken to prevent disease or injury rather than treating it after it occurs. *Example: Simple preventive measures like vaccinations are often underfunded compared to expensive treatments.*
