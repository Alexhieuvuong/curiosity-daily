# The Ancient Roots of Global Trade: How the Indus People Mastered the Sea

> Before the Greeks, before the Romans, a forgotten civilization was already sailing the high seas for trade—and their ports challenge everything we thought we knew about ancient globalization.

## Why this is interesting
Most people assume that long-distance maritime trade began with the Greeks or Phoenicians, but recent archaeological discoveries reveal that the Indus Valley Civilization—often thought to be land-locked—had a thriving network of sea ports and conducted brisk sea-borne trade with Mesopotamia as early as the late third millennium B.C. [4]. This rewrites the timeline of global commerce and shows that the fundamental mechanics of shipping—ports, routes, and bulk cargo—are thousands of years older than we realized.

## First principles
Maritime transport is the movement of goods or people via waterways, and it has been a cornerstone of human logistics since early shipbuilding emerged [1]. The core advantage is simple: water transport is far cheaper per unit of weight than land transport, because ships can carry enormous loads without the constraints of terrain or road infrastructure [1]. The trade-off is speed—ocean shipping is slow—but for non-perishable bulk goods like grain, metals, or timber, the cost savings make it the only viable option for long-distance exchange. A sea lane is simply a regularly used, safe, and economic route across a body of water [2]. The Indus people, by establishing ports, tapped into this same basic economic logic over 4,000 years ago.

## Break it into pieces
- **How did a supposedly "land-locked" civilization become a maritime power?** Recent explorations have brought to light several Harappan ports, giving a coastal aspect to the Indus civilization [4].

- **What evidence do we have?** Archaeologists have uncovered Harappan ports, suggesting a brisk sea-borne trade between the Indus people and the Sumerians [4].

## Follow the incentives
The source material does not describe the specific incentives, risks, or profits of Indus merchants. However, the existence of a trade network implies that the benefits of exchange outweighed the costs for both parties. The Indus people likely sought goods from Mesopotamia, and the Sumerians sought goods from the Indus Valley, with sea transport being more cost-effective than the overland route through Baluchistan [4].

## How it echoes elsewhere
The same pattern appears in the later "Maritime Silk Road" initiative, where China today invests in port infrastructure along sea lanes to boost export trade [5]. The core mechanic—using cheap water transport to connect resource-rich regions with manufacturing or consumption centers—is identical, only the scale and technology have changed. Also, the Greek shipping industry, which now controls the world's largest merchant fleet, follows the same logic: Greek families own and operate ships that carry bulk cargoes like oil and grain across global sea lanes, just as the Indus people carried goods across the Arabian Sea [3].

## A real-world case
Illustrative: The Indus port of Lothal (in modern Gujarat, India) is often cited as one of the best-preserved examples. Excavations there have revealed a dockyard and seals that match those found in Mesopotamia, suggesting a direct trade link around 2500 B.C. [4]. The dock was built with baked bricks and had a lock-gate system to maintain water levels during tides—a sophisticated engineering feat for its time. This port allowed the Indus people to export cotton, beads, and timber to Sumer, and import copper, tin, and precious stones. ## Second-order effects
The existence of Indus maritime trade challenges the traditional narrative that ancient civilizations developed in isolation. It suggests that globalization—the exchange of goods, ideas, and technologies across continents—is not a modern phenomenon but a fundamental human drive. The ports also imply a need for standardized weights, measures, and contracts, which likely accelerated the development of writing and accounting. Furthermore, the decline of the Indus civilization around 1900 B.C. may have been partly due to the collapse of this trade network, as environmental changes made ports unusable and disrupted sea lanes.
