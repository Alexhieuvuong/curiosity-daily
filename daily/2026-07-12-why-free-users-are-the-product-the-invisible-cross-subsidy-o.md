# Why Free Users Are the Product: The Invisible Cross-Subsidy of Two-Sided Platforms

> The reason you don't pay for Facebook isn't corporate generosity—it's a carefully engineered pricing strategy where one group's free ride is another group's profit center.

## Why this is interesting

Most people assume free digital services are a gift of technology or a loss leader that will eventually turn into a paid service. In reality, free is a structural feature of two-sided markets, where platforms deliberately charge one user group nothing—or even pay them—because the other group's willingness to pay depends entirely on that first group's presence. Understanding this inversion of normal pricing reveals why platforms dominate modern economies and why regulators struggle to intervene.

## First principles

A two-sided market is an intermediary that connects two distinct user groups, where the value each group gets depends on the size of the other group [2]. This creates what economists call cross-group network effects: credit card holders want merchants that accept their card, and merchants want cardholders who carry it [2]. The platform's job is to "get both sides on board," which is famously difficult because each side waits for the other—the chicken-and-egg problem [2]. The key insight from economists Rochet, Tirole, Parker, and Van Alstyne is that platforms don't set a single price; they set a *price structure*—how much each side pays—and that structure determines whether the network grows or collapses [2]. A platform can charge one side nothing (or a subsidy) to attract users, then recoup that cost by charging the other side more, because the second side's value comes from accessing the first side [2].

## Break it into pieces

- **Why does one side get free access?** Because their participation creates value for the other side, and the platform can capture that value through higher prices on the paying side. The free side isn't a cost center; it's an asset that generates the paying side's willingness to pay.
- **How is this different from a traditional subsidy?** A grocery store's loss leader (cheap milk) is a temporary tactic to get you in the door to buy profitable items. A platform's free side is a permanent structural feature: the platform would collapse if it started charging that side, because the paying side would lose its reason to pay.
- **Who decides which side pays?** The platform chooses based on which group is more price-sensitive, which group has more substitutes, and which group's participation is more valuable to the other. Typically, the side with more elastic demand (users who would leave if charged) gets the subsidy, while the side with inelastic demand (advertisers who need access) pays.
- **What happens if the platform gets the price structure wrong?** If you charge the free side even a small fee, they leave, the paying side loses its audience, and the network unravels. This is why many platform startups burn cash on user acquisition—they're buying the asset (users) that makes the paying side valuable.

## Follow the incentives

The platform's incentive is to maximize total value created across both sides, not to maximize profit from any single side [2]. For example, Facebook connects users and advertisers: users get free social networking, and advertisers pay to reach those users [2]. The platform's profit depends on having a large user base that attracts advertisers. If users left, advertisers would lose their audience, and the platform's advertising revenue would collapse. The platform's incentive is to keep users happy and growing—hence massive investment in content algorithms, moderation, and engagement features—because users are the asset being sold to advertisers. Advertisers, meanwhile, are willing to pay high prices because the platform offers precise targeting and scale that no other medium can match.

## How it echoes elsewhere

The same cross-subsidy pattern appears in video-game consoles: platforms like Sony sell hardware at competitive prices and recoup through licensing fees from game developers who want access to gamers [2]. Developers pay because gamers are there; gamers get affordable hardware because developers pay. In credit cards, merchants pay interchange fees while cardholders may receive benefits, because merchants value access to cardholders more than cardholders value paying with plastic [2]. In both cases, the side with more concentrated value (developers needing a distribution channel, merchants needing payment acceptance) subsidizes the side with more diffuse value (gamers, shoppers).

## A real-world case

Consider the competition between VHS and Betamax in the 1980s. This was a classic two-sided market battle: consumers wanted the format with more movie titles, and movie studios wanted to release on the format with more consumers [2]. VHS won because it solved the chicken-and-egg problem more effectively, building a larger installed base of players, which attracted more studios to release movies on VHS [2]. Once VHS reached critical mass, the bandwagon effect locked in its dominance [1]. This illustrates that in platform markets, the winner is often not the best product but the one that best manages cross-group network effects and pricing structure.

## Second-order effects

The free-user model creates a fundamental misalignment between platform incentives and user welfare. Because platforms profit from user attention (sold to advertisers), they have incentives to maximize engagement—even if that means promoting addictive content, polarizing material, or violating privacy. Users become the raw material rather than the customer, and the platform's "customer" (the advertiser) has different interests than the user. This explains why data privacy regulation is so contentious: it threatens the asset (user data and attention) that makes the paying side valuable. A second-order effect is market concentration: because network effects create winner-take-most dynamics [1], successful platforms tend toward monopoly, and their pricing structures can become exploitative once they have critical mass. Regulators in the EU have proposed rules to ensure fair competition and worker protections in platform markets [3], but the fundamental tension remains: the free-user model is not a bug—it's the engine.

## A question to sit with

If a platform's free users are the product being sold, what ethical obligations does the platform have toward those users—and can those obligations ever be compatible with maximizing shareholder value?

## Vocabulary Builder

1. **cross-subsidy** — (noun, /krɒs ˈsʌbsɪdi/) — A pricing strategy where profit from one customer group is used to support below-cost pricing for another group. *Example: Facebook's free user access is funded by a cross-subsidy from advertisers.*
2. **two-sided market** — (noun, /tuː ˈsaɪdɪd ˈmɑːkɪt/) — A platform that creates value by facilitating interactions between two distinct user groups. *Example: Credit card networks are a classic two-sided market linking consumers and merchants.*
3. **network effect** — (noun, /ˈnɛtwɜːk ɪˈfɛkt/) — The phenomenon where a product or service becomes more valuable as more people use it. *Example: The telephone exhibits a direct network effect because each new user makes the network more valuable for everyone else.*
4. **chicken-and-egg problem** — (noun, /ˈtʃɪkɪn ænd ɛɡ ˈprɒbləm/) — The dilemma of needing each user group to attract the other, with no group willing to join first. *Example: A new dating platform faces the chicken-and-egg problem of needing users to attract users.*
5. **price structure** — (noun, /praɪs ˈstrʌktʃər/) — The allocation of costs across different sides of a platform, not just the total price. *Example: The platform's price structure charged developers high fees while subsidizing gamers.*
6. **bandwagon effect** — (noun, /ˈbændwæɡən ɪˈfɛkt/) — The tendency for adoption to accelerate rapidly once a product reaches critical mass. *Example: Once VHS reached enough users, the bandwagon effect locked in its dominance over Betamax.*
7. **critical mass** — (noun, /ˈkrɪtɪkəl mæs/) — The minimum number of users needed for a network to become self-sustaining. *Example: A social network must reach critical mass before network effects can drive organic growth.*
8. **interoperability** — (noun, /ˌɪntərˌɒpərəˈbɪləti/) — The ability of different systems or platforms to work together and exchange data. *Example: Platforms resist interoperability because it weakens the network effects that protect their dominance.*
9. **elastic demand** — (noun, /ɪˈlæstɪk dɪˈmænd/) — Demand that is highly sensitive to price changes. *Example: Users have more elastic demand than advertisers, so platforms charge users less.*
10. **winner-take-most** — (adjective, /ˈwɪnər teɪk moʊst/) — A market outcome where the leading firm captures the vast majority of profits. *Example: Network effects often create winner-take-most dynamics in platform industries.*
11. **complementor** — (noun, /ˌkɒmplɪˈmɛntər/) — A firm that provides complementary products or services that enhance a platform's value. *Example: App developers are complementors who increase the value of a smartphone platform.*
12. **externality** — (noun, /ˌɛkstɜːˈnælɪti/) — A cost or benefit that affects a third party who did not choose to incur it. *Example: A new user joining a social network creates a positive externality for existing users.*
